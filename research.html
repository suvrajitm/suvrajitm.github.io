Continuum of conformational states from cryo-EM, via manifold learning

Beyond discrete classes — the “space of images” as a window into molecular motion.

(a) ManifoldEM. Conventional maximum-likelihood classification of cryo-EM projection images recovers a small number of discrete conformations. ManifoldEM aims instead at the continuum — the energy landscape of conformational states — given enough projections of the molecular machine (Dashti et al. 2014; Frank et al. 2016). The technique analyzes the “space of images” through manifold-learning methods.

(b) Propagating reaction coordinates across angular space. A central step in deriving a consolidated occupancy and energy landscape is propagating reaction coordinates from a known anchor projection direction — historically done by hand. We developed a method (Maji et al. 2020, J. Chem. Inf. Model., Frontiers in CryoEM Modeling) that solves the propagation problem with a probabilistic graphical model and feature-extraction primitives such as optical flow and histograms of oriented gradients, demonstrated on a ryanodine receptor and on the ribosome.

Project · 2017 · published in JPC-B

Domain motion via moments of inertia

Quantitative, reproducible, comparable descriptions of how domains rotate and translate.

Molecular machines — ribosomes, for instance — perform their biophysical work through ordered conformational changes, often described as relative motions of their domains. We typically encounter such motion as a series of structural snapshots: PDB models from flexible fitting of cryo-EM densities, X-ray crystallography, or molecular-dynamics trajectories.

We developed a method (Maji et al. 2017, implemented on the VMD platform) that quantifies these motions in a general, biologically meaningful way: each domain’s motion is characterized as a coordinate transformation using moments of inertia tensors (cf. Agirrezabala et al. 2011, 2012). The toolset gives a reproducible segmentation of domains and a generalized description of their motion via principal axes of inertia — recovering rotation angle, native rotation axis, and the projection of rotation onto any chosen reference axis. The result: domain-motion data that can be compared across studies and groups.

→ Quantitative Characterization of Domain Motions in Molecular Machines · DOI 10.1021/acs.jpcb.6b10732

Earlier work

Single-molecule biophysics & super-resolution imaging

Before joining the Frank Lab, my interests centered on single-molecule biophysics and fluorescence-microscopy image analysis: single-particle tracking of biological molecules, super-resolution bioimaging, and 3D single-molecule localization using fluorescent beads, quantum dots, and fluorogen-activating peptides (FAPs). My main thread there was a generative-modeling approach for inferring biological structure from single-molecule super-resolution datasets — a route to improving dynamic localization imaging in cells.

In parallel, I worked on co-translational protein folding — computationally studying how codon usage and tRNA concentration shape translation rates across proteins.
